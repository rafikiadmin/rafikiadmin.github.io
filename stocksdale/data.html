My Data Vision

Today everyone loves data and loves talking about data and data goes well with everything: Warehouses, Lakes, Science, Decision-Making, etc. “Machine learning” and “neural networks” enable us to crunch massive amounts of data to gain intelligence and enable effective visualization and communication. Somehow, though, we still struggle to get, work with, and understand the very information we need to do so much of what we want to do. Why?

We humans are meaning-makers. Unlike a digital camera sensor detecting any light that hits it, we are constantly filtering, attending to, transforming and organizing the information that bombards our eyes and ears. I think we should consider the same approach for our technology systems and solutions. Since storage space has become cheap, we tend collect everything we possibly can and worry about making sense of it all later. Sometimes that is appropriate and valuable, but not always. In many cases, it is better to architect storage solutions that organize and structure our data so that it makes sense and we can avoid excessive “processing” and “changing” later.

Specifically, I like architecting solutions that organize data into Entities and Relationships. I prefer to think of data as either immutable “entities” or time-based (event-based) relationships between entities.

What this means in practice is that the attributes we associate directly with an Entity type rarely, if ever, change. (For example, if a person is an entity type, things like name, birthdate, gender, etc. mostly do not change.) Other things “about” that entity might change, but those things are probably are not directly associated with (or “belong” to) the entity. Instead, they are time-based relationships to other entities. Think of a job as one simple example: Joe (entity) worked as (relationship) Lead Programmer (entity) from June 1985 to July 1990 (time). Joe’s current relationships can be structured the same way (Entity – Relationship – Entity – time), with the only difference that they have a start date-time without an end date-time.

While I am disinclined to take an absolutist/purist approach to any problem (reality has a way of intruding on our best plans), I generally think data can fit into one of these two structures and it can be an extremely powerful way to manage data. In its most pure form, this concept could be used to build unique write-only systems that have their own data tracking and logging because the data never really “changes”, it just naturally becomes historical as end date-times are assigned.

These concepts are understood by both technical and non-technical users alike. Thinking in terms of entities and relationships allows intelligent discussion about solutions among all developers and stakeholders. And sometimes, simply allowing users to consume data in these structured ways is enough to help them become effective in their work.

Even if constraints prevent us from building data systems this way, simply thinking about our most important entities and relationships is beneficial to design and engineering processes. Defining core entities and relationships can provide models for re-envisioning even data that is not currently structured that way. That can lead to user interfaces, reporting, analysis, automation, and data consumption and distribution systems that are quicker to build, easier to maintain, and more intuitive for users.

Rafiki Systems, my failed effort at a new school software platform, was not designed to replace existing school solutions as much as it was to wrap a better model around existing data. The vision was to allow existing, less ideal, but extremely useful legacy systems to continue as normal, while new development – including a portal to display that legacy data – could be built faster and in ways that worked better for the users.
